Reality check on what you have today
Your current stack is strong on tax appraisal attributes + storm signals + Texas business “unmasking”, but it is weak where the money and legal truth live: recorded ownership / liens / foreclosure activity / litigation / compliance-grade decision-maker identification. In other words, you’ve built a solid “lead hint engine,” not a defensible “ownership and control intelligence system.” That distinction matters if you want to scale without getting destroyed by data quality disputes, complaints, or platform blocks.

Your property backbone is the Dallas Central Appraisal District ArcGIS-driven data. Appraisal data is useful for segmentation and valuation, but it is not the canonical legal record of ownership changes, liens, or creditor control—those are recorded through county clerk/recorder systems. Dallas County’s own Recording Division explicitly describes recording deeds and documents associated with deeds (including liens, releases of lien, transfers, probate-related property matters). 

Your storm layer is credible: the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration Storm Events Database and SWDI/NEXRAD-derived products are official sources for severe weather reporting and radar-derived hail signatures. NOAA’s Storm Events Database states it contains data from January 1950 through October 2025 (as of the site’s current update), which is much deeper than your current “2020–present” posture. 

Your decision-maker enrichment is ambitious—but you’re currently mixing high-reliability public records (state tax accounts, permits) with high-fragility / high-risk scraping targets (SOS scraping, parts of the open web, some exposure via WHOIS/RDAP). Also, your “LLC chain tracing” can easily become a false-confidence trap in Texas because formation data often identifies initial managers/members and registered agents—not necessarily the true economic owners in any enduring or complete sense. Texas SOS Form 205 instructions, for example, require listing the LLC’s initial managers or initial members depending on management structure; that is not the same as continuously updated beneficial ownership. 

Finally, you have outbound triggers (pre-storm SMS alerts, lead workflows). If you’re contacting individuals at scale, your data strategy and compliance strategy are inseparable: U.S. rules around calls/texts and consent are not a footnote; they can become existential risk (TCPA, DNC, CAN-SPAM). 

The biggest missing layer is recorded ownership, encumbrances, and distress
If you care about “owners and decision makers,” you cannot skip the recorder/clerks. Right now, you’re relying heavily on appraisal-district owner fields and state entity lookups. That leaves you blind to the documents that actually control property outcomes:

Deeds, liens, releases, probate transfers, and real ownership timelines
County recording offices are where deeds and lien-related documents are recorded; Dallas County’s Recording Division states it records property deeds and documents associated with the deed such as liens, transfers, probate matters, and releases of lien. 
Dallas County’s Online Record Search hub links to official portals for “UCC / Personal Property / Deeds,” and the related public search portal for official records notes it is “Certified through 02/17/2026,” which is exactly the sort of recency stamp you want for evidence-driven ownership timelines.

Foreclosure notices and tax sale pipelines
You’re missing a major “distress → decision-maker urgency” channel:

Dallas County’s County Clerk Recording Division answers “Where can I find out which properties will be sold?” by stating notices are posted on the County Clerk site and that notices must be posted 21 days prior to the sale. 
Dallas County Public Works’ Property Division page describes tax sale properties sold via the Sheriff through a live online auction held the first Tuesday of every month and links to lists and process details. 
Dallas County also provides a Property Tax Lookup/Payment application for accessing tax info, and publishes delinquency guidance and delinquency deadlines—useful for a “delinquency risk” feature set. 
This is not optional for a serious lead engine. Distressed owners behave differently; they also have clearer “who is making decisions right now” signals (attorneys, trustees, lenders, tax sale notices).

UCC filings and creditor control signals
UCC is not “property ownership,” but it is often control and leverage—especially for commercial operations and business assets tied to a site.

Texas SOS now routes UCC filings through an SOS Portal system (paper filings no longer accepted as of Aug. 29, 2025 per SOS UCC guidance), and SOSDirect indicates a per-search fee structure. 

Your “free info” expectation needs to be corrected here: in Texas, some of the most valuable legal signals are accessible but not “free at scale.”

Your hazard model is leaving accuracy and expansion on the table
Your hail pipeline is credible, but it has two big weaknesses: temporal short-sightedness and spatial imprecision.

You’re artificially limiting historic storm coverage
NOAA’s Storm Events Database states the dataset spans January 1950 to October 2025 (current as posted). Your “2020–present” constraint is a choice, not a limitation of the source. 

A five-year view: insurance/roofing players with the best loss modeling will use long-run climatology plus radar-derived swaths; short windows get outcompeted.

Your 5-mile “hail proximity” matching is crude
Storm Events reports are not the same as actual hail footprint. NOAA’s Severe Weather Data Inventory (SWDI) is explicitly designed as an integrated severe weather archive, with multiple access methods and machine formats—including CSV/JSON—and includes NEXRAD Level III derived products (including hail-related products). 

NOAA’s WDTD “MESH Tracks” description ties directly to Maximum Estimated Size of Hail (MESH), a radar-derived maximum hail size metric over time windows. 

What you should add

Hail swath/track-based exposure rather than circle buffers
exposure intensity over time (max MESH, duration above threshold)
uncertainty fields (radar coverage gaps, algorithm confidence where available)
You’re missing non-hail property-risk overlays customers will expect
If you sell to anyone serious (commercial roofing, asset managers), they’ll ask: flood, wildfire, wind. Flood is the quickest win.

The Federal Emergency Management Agency National Flood Hazard Layer (NFHL) is a geospatial database of effective flood hazard data; FEMA provides web/map services and ArcGIS REST access for mapping and queries. 

With NFHL you can compute:

flood zone classification per parcel/building footprint
proximity to floodways
“risk stacking” features (hail + flood-prone = different buyer urgency and insurance angle)
Physical-asset intelligence from imagery and footprints
Your “roof age” scoring is structurally weak if it relies mainly on year built or appraisal fields. You need better proxies based on permits and imagery.

Free/public building geometry and imagery resources include:

Microsoft’s U.S. building footprints dataset (computer-generated footprints, freely available for download and use). 
NAIP aerial imagery: USGS describes NAIP as 1-meter ground sample distance for the U.S. (for many years of coverage) and The National Map’s NAIP Plus service provides orthoimagery with resolutions from 6 inches to 1 meter depending on source. 
Even basic footprint + imagery usage unlocks:

roof area estimation (better than square footage proxies)
roof complexity heuristics
visible damage pre/post storm (where resolution supports it)
“construction change detection” when paired with permits

Assumed names (DBA) are a major missing “operator identity” layer
You’re trying to identify tenants and responsible parties. In Texas, assumed name filings are a key public mechanism for mapping an operating trade name to real parties.

Dallas County Clerk guidance for assumed names explicitly instructs searching County Assumed Names records (via its public search portal) and notes filing procedures. 

Tarrant County similarly states an assumed name/DBA should be filed with the County Clerk’s office in the county where business is conducted. 

That matters because your “sales tax permit holder at address” logic doesn’t catch:

entities operating under DBAs without clean tax-location matching
management companies branded differently from the owner LLC
Your Texas SOS and Comptroller assumptions need correction
SOSDirect is not free: Texas SOSDirect indicates a $1 statutorily authorized fee per search. 
Texas Form 205 instructions show you get initial manager/member names/addresses depending on structure—not a continuously updated, complete ownership ledger. 
Beneficial owner reporting under the CTA is submitted to FinCEN (not to the Texas SOS). Texas SOS explicitly points BOI reporting to FinCEN’s system. 
Texas Comptroller public-data APIs require an API key (the documentation shows x-api-key as required in headers and points to registration to obtain one). 
If your product messaging is “free public records,” you need to differentiate:

free to view manually vs
free to automate at scale vs
free to redistribute commercially
WHOIS/RDAP is not the enrichment goldmine it used to be
You’re doing WHOIS/RDAP lookups for domain registrant info. That’s increasingly unreliable for personal attribution.

ICANN states that gTLD registries/registrars are required to provide RDAP services using the gTLD RDAP Profile and that as of Jan. 28, 2025 they are no longer required to provide WHOIS services (with limited exceptions). That environment is deeply shaped by GDPR-era data minimization and access constraints. 

What you’ll often get now is proxy/redacted registration, which is weak for identifying real humans.

U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development publishes a Multifamily Assistance & Section 8 database that includes contract-level and property-level tables and provides downloads (property-level requires linking via property id). 

Texas Department of Housing and Community Affairs provides participating properties information for programs like Section 811 PRA (map/list). 

Even if this isn’t your whole market, it helps you:

Compliance, scaling, and a ruthless roadmap
Here’s the uncomfortable truth: the path you’re on naturally evolves into either (a) a compliant, defensible data business, or (b) a fragile scraping operation that gets blocked and generates complaints. If you want to win in 5–10 years, you build (a).

The compliance trapdoors in your current approach
If you generate leads and contact people, you must treat communications compliance as core product infrastructure:

The Federal Communications Commission states the TCPA generally requires consent before certain calls/texts using autodialers or artificial/prerecorded voice, and FCC rules require prior express written consent for certain categories. 
The Federal Trade Commission describes the National Do Not Call Registry and related Telemarketing Sales Rule obligations, including restrictions on calling numbers on the registry or a seller’s DNC list. 
FTC’s CAN-SPAM guidance states CAN-SPAM sets rules for commercial email and gives recipients the right to have you stop emailing them. 
At the state level, if you ever cross into “data broker” style aggregation, California’s deletion mechanism ecosystem is a preview of what broader U.S. regulation can look like:

California Privacy Protection Agency describes DROP as an accessible deletion mechanism for consumers to request deletion from registered data brokers, with regulations adopted Sept. 26, 2025.
